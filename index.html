I think I can say with a fair amount confidence that everybody uses YouTube, most pretty regularly. And because building a web app that would interest a wide range of people was very appealing to me, I thought an app that integrated YouTube would be just the thing to build. Retro YouTube was a project where I wanted to not only use the YouTube API, but use it in a way I hadn't seen before. I wanted to display YouTube videos in an entirely different way and change the way the User interacts with the videos. This led me to display the video results on a 3D model of a old fashioned television. In order to stay consistent with the idea of TV, I decided users would interact with videos by using their phone as a remote, which renders via media queries when the site is loaded on mobile.

Yes, it sounds like a dating site, but it's actually an app that aims to connect tennis players with each other. I have loved tennis for a long time, but growing up in Bardstown, KY, it was always a challenge to find new people to play matches with, outside of high school competitions. Everybody tended to stick with the two or three people they usually played with. There was an ample pool of talented players from all ages, but there was nothing connecting them. Match-Er tries to solve that problem by giving users the ability to friend nearby tennis players, send and receive invitations to matches, arrange the time and place, save the scores of completed matches, and track their overall record.

Task-Timer started out as timer for Rubik's cubes that would keep record of all solve times. However, I quickly realized that this idea wouldn't appeal to, let alone be useful to, most people, so I reworked it as a timer for tasks people like me occupy themselves with. Users select a task from a dropdown such as checking email, catching up on news, or studying and press "Start." A timer runs until the user decides to stop their task. After stopping the task, the user is able to make notes on what they specifically they worked on, or what they accomplished. The task's title, time, date, and notes are saved and can be viewed along with other previous tasks. Parts of the development of this app, such as building a timer with JavaScript, were challenging but enjoyable.

About Me

My real name is John, but everyone calls me Jack. You can also call me a fullstack developer who loves what he does. How could I not? After all, things are never boring in a developer's day as new challenges and complicated problems are commonplace. But, also commonplace, is the joy of solving those complicated problems and the sense of accomplishment when a project is finished. I thoroughly enjoy learning new frameworks and libraries that build and improve my skills and allow me to see aspects of development from a different perspective. I am rarely, if ever, fully content with my knowledge and skills, because I know I can always be learning and improving.

Away from coding, I tend to keep busy with many different activities and hobbies. They range from the Boston Red Sox, to my violin, to Rubik's cubes, to playing tennis or going on a hike, to getting a drink with friends, to just sitting back with a book. I don't like to be stuck doing the same thing all the time, and I believe that's why I love being a web developer: the possibilites of what one can build are never ending.
